## INTRODUCTION 
### BACKGROUND 
1. Singapore is situated at the cross-roads of major shipping lanes linking the east and west and is next to the Strait of Singapore that serves as an important route for Very Large Crude Carriers (VLCCs) travelling to and from the Far East. The Port of Singapore had 96,857vessel arrivals totalling 2,903 million gross tons in 2020 and tankers accounted for about 27% of the total number of vessels calling at our port. In addition, 49.8 million tonnes of bunker sales were reported in 2020 and the annual sales figures have been increasing over the years. Therefore, the Port of Singapore’s exposure to pollution risk is higher than other ports in the world. 
2. The Straits of Singapore lies between Singapore and the Riau Archipelago Indonesia and links the Straits of Malacca to the South China Sea. It is approximately 113km long and 5 to 12km wide, although its narrowest navigable width is 2.9km. It is on the shipping route of the Asia Pacific region, linking East Asia to Europe. This makes it one of the busiest sea lanes in the world hence, highly vulnerable to oil spills. Furthermore, besides being one of the world’s busiest container ports, Singapore is also a marine bunkering centre, as well as an oil refining centre with a high volume of marine trade and shipping traffic daily. According to the 1999 Oil Spill Intellgence Report, the Straits of Singapore is considered to be one of the world’s hot spots for oil spills. Since 1960, Singapore and Malaysia together have suffered 39 spills totalling at least 24 tonnes. 
3. Recognising the high vulnerability of the Straits to incidents leading to oil spills and subsequent pollution of surrounding waters and shores, the Maritime and Port Authority of Singapore (MPA) has put in place a comprehensive system to ensure navigational safety in the Straits. This is to minimise marine accidents and oil pollution. MPA is the responsible national authority for regulating and controlling oil spill clean up operations within the Singapore Port Limits and the approaches to the port. In order to facilitate the performance of this obligation, the Authority is responsible for formulation of an Oil Spill Contingency Plan (OSCP) to coordinate and control the clean-up operations. 
4. The OSCP caters for marine oil spills due to the following scenarios:\
a. Marine incidents involving passing vessels in the Singapore Straits;\
b. Marine incidents involving vessels in port;\
c. Damage of to underwater pipelines;\
d. Unknown source of oil slick found within the Singapore Port Limits and the approaches to the port.
### PURPOSE & OBJECTIVES 
5. This is a contingency plan to deal with marine oil spill within the Singapore Port Limits and the approaches to the port. The objective of this plan is to ensure prompt and effective response to an oil spill by:\
a. Identifying priority (oil sensitive) areas for protection and clean-up in the event of a spill;\
b. Pre-determining high risk areas in the plan;\
c. Establishing a response organisation (including lines of communications and other procedures) comprising government agencies, oil industry, spill response companies, terminal operators and vessels;\
d. and Providing a system of mobilising and deploying adequate manpower and equipment in spill response.
### SCOPE & GEOGRAPHIC BOUNDARY 
6. This plan covers the response to spillage of oil within Singapore Port Limits and the approaches to the port.

## PART 1 – STRATEGY & PLANNING 
### RESPONSE SYSTEM & POLICY
### 1. **National Response System**
a. **Lead Agency**. MPA is the lead agency for marine oil spill clean-up within the Singapore Port Limits and the approaches to the port.\
b. **Response Policy**. The objectives of an oil spill response are to:\
(1) Protect human health and safety;\
(2) Minimize environmental impacts; and\
(3) Restore the environment, as near as is practicable, to pre-spill conditions.\
c. **Principal Considerations for OSCP**.\
(1) The response actions are dependent on the type of oil, size and location of spill, prevailing sea and weather conditions at the spill site, and the environmental sensitivity of the coastline/site impacted.\
(2) The owners of respective waterfront facilities are responsible for the provision of the first layer of protection for facilities under their charge.\
(3) There is a need to balance the ecological, recreational and commercial concerns in an oil spill response in view of the resource constraints.\
(4) For a major spill, MPA will respond by mobilising and deploying all available manpower from MPA and responding agencies. According to the International Tanker Owners Pollution Ferderation Limited, a non-profit organisation dealing with oil and chemical spills, oil spills that exceed 700 tonnes can be categorised as large spills.\
d. **Levels of Response**. The concept of tiered response is adopted in this plan. It links the credible spill scenarios to attainable scales of response and enables escalation from one tiered response to another should the need arises. In addition, it provides a practical basis for planning of a spill response in terms of required resources and likely environmental impact. The three levels of tiered response are based on the following spill scenarios:\
(1) **Tier 1 (Minor Spill)** – A minor spill that can dealt by the concerned stakeholders (individual vessel's or terminal's in-house) capability by using the resources at a specific incident or facility would be deemed sufficient to cope with the situation.\
(2) **Tier 2 (Medium Spill)** – A medium spill that may require mutual assistance of oil spill response resources. A more serious incident which may involve a wider extent of contamination and the involvement of a number of different government agencies and other stakeholders.\
(3) **Tier 3 (Larger Spill)** – A larger spill beyond the capacity of Tier 2 or catastrophic spill that would require Regional assistance. An incident of national significance, with the potential for wide‐scale impacts and the requirement for an extensive multi-agency emergency response, resources from throughout Singapore and potentially from outside the country.\
PTMA (MPA Port Master) or his designate will authorise the level of response after consulting the MPA Crisis Management Group (CMG) led by CE MPA. 
### 2. National Response Options and Priorities.
The order for response options should be prioritised based on the circumstances of the spill and conditions prevailing such as the weather conditions and currents, type of oil and time needed to deploy resources to the scene. The circumstances of each incident will be evaluated before mobilising equipment or other resources. If appropriate, dispersants can be considered for application onto the spilled oil at the onset of the spill so as to maximise the clean-up works within the “window of opportunity”. The following response options could be considered in the event of an oil spill at sea.\
“Window of opportunity” is the length of time for which dispersant use is effective and is closely linked to the rate at which an oil ‘weathers’. It can be as short as an hour or less for heavy oils or as long as several days or more for light crude oils.\
**a. Prevent or reduce further spillage.** If a vessel is involved and is continuing to spill the oil, the suggested first response will be to prevent or reduce further spillage as follows:\
(1) MPA Port Operations Control Centre (POCC) / Marine Safety Control Centre (MSCC) is to advise master of the casualty ship to anchor at the nearest safe location away from shipping route if the ship is able to move on her own power as soon as possible to localise the oil spill;\
(2) The master of the ship will be asked to identify the damaged tanks and, if possible, to transfer the oil contained in those tanks to any other space available on board. This may be difficult if the bottom of the tank concerned is damaged;\
(3) Transfer some or all of the remaining oil to another ship or ships to stop the outflow oil and to allow the vessel to proceed to a suitable berth for complete discharge; and\
(4) If the vessel is aground in a position that no other ship can go alongside, it may be possible to use a shallow draught barge or floating storage tank or a dracone to remove part of the oil.\
**b. Monitor and wait.** This will be the better decision if the oil is not threading shoreward, or if no important resources are threatened, or if the oil is breaking up naturally, or if conditions are such that positive response options are not practicable. With this strategy, the movement of the oil slick will be monitored with preparedness to respond. Monitoring the movement of the slick can be done by:\
(1) Aerial surveillance - Aerial observation is essential for an effective response to oil spills, both to monitor the movements and fate of the oil and also to improve control of clean-up operations. Surveillance from the air will be conducted by trained observers who can identify oil on the water surface visually or by using electronic sensors.\
(2) Computer tracking forecasts – the OILMAP, a modelling software located at the MSCC can be used to predict the movements of the oil slick. While the modelling tool is a guide, it will be used to supplement the aerial surveillance efforts.\
**c. Contain and recover the spilled oil.**\
(1) This being the most common response strategy because of environmental and socio-economic reasons, entails physically removing the oil from the surface of the water. It is also the strategy that employs the most logistics both at sea and ashore. However, its effectiveness is hampered by bad weather or rough sea conditions.\
(2) Ideally it is to recover the bulk of the spilled oil before it is stranded but this may be very difficult to achieve as where oil has spread over a very large area of open water, even the largest containment and recovery system are tiny compared to the size of the spill.\
(3) Containment and recovery will be done as follows:\
(a) deploy containment booms to prevent spreading and concentrate the spilled oil;\
(b) deploy skimmers to recover the boomed oil; and\
(c) transfer the recovered oil into barges to be transported to shore storage, disposal or treatment.\
(4) This strategy will be mounted as close to the source of the spill as soon as possible before the spread enlarges. Once spreading has been allowed to continue, it can become impractical and expensive to contain and recover the oil.\
(5) In adopting this response technique for combating oil spill, the following steps are recommended:\
(a) assess the merits of oil recovery options at sea and near shore against the prevailing conditions such as sea state, wind, water movements and the location of areas needing protection;\
(b) consider the type of oil to be recovered, its viscosity at ambient temperatures and any change with time;\
(c) then select an appropriate skimmer from the types available against the criteria of capacity, reliability, robustness, field performance, weight, handling, versatility, power source, maintenance and cost;\
(d) identify the availability of vacuum trucks and other suction systems that may be employed for recovering thick layers of oil on waters;\
(e) use aircraft for monitoring and controlling oil recovery operations at sea;\
(f) monitor skimmer performance continuously to ensure optimum efficiency; and\
(g) ensure that the logistics of pumping, storing and disposing of recovered oil are addressed.\
(6) Containment and recovery systems are classified as static or dynamic. Static systems rely on oil drifting into them whilst dynamic ones actively pursue the spilled oil.\
**d. Apply dispersants.**\
(1) The use of dispersant to deal with oil spills is an aid to the process of natural dispersion. Dispersants actually do not remove the oil. The dispersants approved for use in Singapore Waters and guidelines on the use of dispersants to treat oil slicks at sea are detailed in Annex A and Annex B respectively.\
(2) These dispersant chemicals work by increasing the surface area of the spilled oil by producing very small droplets thereby enhancing biodegradation by naturally occurring micro-organisms in the oceans waters.\
(3) The most important point to note in using dispersant for combating oil spills is the speed of response. Too long a time lapse will expose the spilled oil to weathering, subsequently affecting the effect of the dispersant on it.\
(4) It is also important to recognise the limitations of dispersants, particularly their inability to treat very viscous oils and water-in-oil emulsions.\
(5) Dispersants when used in some areas will cause detrimental consequences to the marine environment rather than preventing or reducing the environmental impact of the spill. In Singapore, an inter agency committee led by NEA has pre-approved the specification of the types of dispersant to be used in Singapore waters based on efficacy and toxicity (see Annex A).\
(6) For beach cleaning, dispersants should only be used after the gross pollution has been removed. Care must be taken to prevent the penetration of oil into beach material and to reduce damage to marine life caused by prolonged exposure to the dispersant / oil mixtures.\
(7) Effective application of dispersants can be achieved through spraying from surface vessels or aircraft or land-based equipment at the dose rate recommended by the manufacturer. While surface vessels are suitable for dealing with small spills close to the port, aircraft potentially offer a more cost-effective response for larger spills offshore.\
(8) The location of dispersant stockpiles (see Annex C) shall be referred to in order to effectively deploy the stockpiles nearest to the spill site.\
(9) Dispersant is usually effective in water depths above 10m.\
**e. Protection of key resources.**\
(1) One of the most effective uses of containment booms is to protect or deflect the oil away from vulnerable resources such as water intakes, marinas and ecologically important areas. The logistical support, training, weather constraints, capital investment etc., are invariably reduced when booms are used in this type of defensive mode. The booming plans for some of the key facilities prepared by OSRL and TTS are detailed in Annex F.\
(2) In adopting the technique to protect key resources, the following steps are recommended to be taken:\
(a) Determine priorities for protection based on statutory / organisational business continuity requirements;\
(b) Decide whether selected areas are to be protected by either towed or moored booms;\
(c) Obtain as much information as possible on currents, tides and winds;\
(d) Calculate forces likely to be exerted on the booms;\
(e) Select suitable points for mooring or means for towing;\
(f) Consider reliability, ease, speed of deployment and arrangements for suitable storage, maintenance and repair;\
(g) Review designs available and select booms that best meet the needs;\
(h) Thoroughly train personnel and maintain their standards by practical exercises; and\
(i) Appreciate the limitations of booms in containing the oil and be prepared to improvise when the need arises.\
**f. Shoreline clean-up.** The decision to conduct clean-up of affected shoreline will depend on factors as follows:\
(1) Impact of stranded oil on environment;\
(2) Impact of stranded oil on commercial activities;\
(3) Possibilities that stranded oil might re-contaminate another part of the shoreline;\
(4) Feasibility of clean-up operations;\
(5) In some instances, oil can be deliberately allowed to come ashore to 'sacrificial' beaches or 'enhanced collection areas' for subsequent collection;\
(6) Cleaning of shorelines is usually carried out in two stages:\
a. Primary phase - consists of removal of floating oil and heavy contamination as soon as possible in order to avoid further pollution.\
b. Final cleaning phase - to remove the final traces of oil and oily stains.\
**g. Combination of the above.** A combination of the above strategies can be employed to tackle a spill. The source of the spilled oil will firstly be isolated followed by natural clean-up or by the use of dispersants on the spill at sea and shorelines clean-up.

OSRL has 3 dedicated fast response oil spill vessels specially built to combat a marine oil spill in near shore and harbour waters. It has a boat dispersant spraying capability and can also do oil spill containment and recovery if required.\
(2) T&T Salvage (TTS)\
(a) TTS has bought over SOSRC which is a fully operational oil spill clean-up company. Like OSRL, the sub business unit houses equipment and dispersants for cleaning up oil spills.\
(3) Singapore Salvage Engineers Pte Ltd (SSE)\
(a) SSE is a fully operational oil spill clean-up company which houses equipment and dispersants for cleaning up oil spills.\
(b) SSE is MPA’s contractor to carry out containment and recovery of spilled oil during the oil spill clean-up operations
### 5. Role of Shipowners 
**a. Under Section 17(1) of the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea Act, 1991,** if any refuse, waste matter, garbage, plastics, marine pollutant in packaged form or trade effluent is discharged from any ship into Singapore waters or into any part of the sea or waters outside Singapore waters but subsequently drifts or flows into Singapore waters, the owner of that ship shall be liable for the costs of any measure reasonably taken by MPA, after the discharge for the purpose of removing it and for preventing or reducing any damage caused in Singapore by contamination resulting from the discharge.\
**b. Under Section 18 of the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea Act, 1991,** if any oil, oily mixture or noxious liquid substance is discharged from any ship into Singapore waters or into any part of the sea or waters outside Singapore waters and the oil, mixture or substance subsequently flows or drifts into Singapore waters, the owner of the ship shall be liable for the costs of any measures reasonably taken by MPA after the discharge for the purpose of removing it and for preventing or reducing damage caused in Singapore by contamination resulting from the discharge.\
**c. Under Section 19 of the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea Act, 1991,** if any oil, oily mixture or noxious liquid substance is discharged from any place on land or from any apparatus used for transferring the oil, mixture or substance from and to any ship (whether to or from a place on land or to and from another ship) or for any other operation, the person in charge of that apparatus or the occupier of that place, as the case may be, shall be liable to pay for the costs of any reasonable measures taken by MPA after the discharge in removing or eliminating the oil, mixture or substance.\
**d. Under Section 20 of the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea Act, 1991,** any person who puts, throws, casts or deposits or causes to be put, thrown, cast or deposited into Singapore waters any oil, oily mixture, refuse, garbage, plastics, waste matter, carcasses, marine pollutant in packaged form, noxious liquid substances or trade effluent shall be liable to pay for the costs of reasonable measures taken by MPA in removing or eliminating it.\
**e. Under Section 3 of the Merchant Shipping (Civil Liability and Compensation for Oil Pollution) Act, 1998,** where as a result of any occurrence taking place while a ship is carrying a cargo of oil in bulk, any oil carried by the ship (whether as part of the cargo or otherwise) is discharged or escapes from the ship, the owner of the ship shall be liable, except as otherwise provided by the Act, be liable for:\
(1) any damage caused outside the ship in the territory of Singapore by contamination resulting from the discharge or escape\
(2) the cost of any measures reasonably taken after the discharge or escape for the purpose of preventing or reducing any damage so caused in the territory of Singapore by contamination resulting from the discharge or escape; and\
(3) any damage caused in the territory of Singapore by any measures so taken.\
**f. Besides civil liabilities, spillers also face criminal liabilities as follows:**\
(1) Under Section 3 of the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea Act, 1991, if any oil or oily mixture is discharged into Singapore waters from any place on land, or from any apparatus used for transferring oil from or to any ship (whether to or from a place on land or to or from another ship)\
(a) if the discharge is from a place on land, the occupier of that place or if the discharge is caused by the act of another person who is in that place without the permission (express or implied) of the occupier, that person; or\
(b) if the discharge is from an apparatus use for transferring oil from or to a ship, the person in charge of the apparatus, shall be guilty of an offence and shall be liable on conviction to a fine not less than $1000 and not more than $1 million or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years or to both.\
(2) Under Section 5 of the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea Act, 1991, any person who puts, throws, casts or deposits into Singapore waters, or causes to be put, thrown, cast or deposited thereinto, any oil, oily mixture, refuse, garbage, plastics, waste matter, carcass, noxious liquid substances, marine pollutant in packaged form or trade effluent, shall be guilty of an offence and shall be liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years or to both.\
(3) Under Section 6 of the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea Act, 1991, if any disposal or discharge of refuse, garbage, waste matter, trade effluent, plastics or marine pollutant in packaged form occurs from any ship into Singapore waters, the master, the owner and the agent of the ship shall each be guilty of an offence and shall each be liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years or to both.\
(4) Under Section 7 of the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea Act, 1991, if any discharge of oil or an oily mixture occurs from a Singapore ship into any part of the sea or from any ship into Singapore waters, the master, the owner and the agent of the ship shall each be guilty of an offence and shall each be liable on conviction to a fine not less than $1000 and not more than $1 million or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years or to both.\
(5) Under Section 8 of the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea Act, 1991, if any oil residues that cannot be discharged from a Singapore ship into the sea without contravening section 7 are not retained on board the ship, the master and the owner of the ship shall each be guilty of an offence and shall each be liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding $1million.\
(6) Under Reg 65 of the Maritime and Port Authority of Singapore (Port) Regulations, 1997, no person shall, without the permission of the Authority\
(a) throw, discharge or deposit; or\
(b) cause to be thrown, discharged or deposited any ashes, solid ballast, sludge or any other matter into the waters of the port and may be fined up to a maximum of $5000 under Reg 78(b) of the same Regulations.\
**g. Spillers shall also inform MPA without any delay,** as required under the provisions of the Reporting of Pollution Incident Regulations, 1991. If possible, they shall follow MPA’s policy of spill response and initiate the clean-up operations before MPA’s assistance arrives. 
### 6. International Cooperation & Arrangements 
**a. ASEAN Oil Spill Response Action Plan (OSRAP).** This is an action plan drawn up between ASEAN countries in response to the OPRC Convention. Member countries operate through their National Lead Agency. MPA is the national lead agency of Singapore. MPA Marine Environment and Safety Department is the contact point. Only when a catastrophic spill requires assistance from other ASEAN countries or when a spill affects other ASEAN countries that MPA would activate the OSRAP.\
**b. The Revolving Fund Committee (RFC) Standard Operating Procedures (SOP) for the Joint Oil Spill Combat in the Straits of Malacca and Singapore.** In the event of an oil spill requiring regional efforts within the Malacca and Singapore Straits, MPA shall effectively assess the situation and decide on the activation of the RFC SOP.

### 8. Clean-up Cost Recovery 
**a. Documentations, bills and claims.**\
(1) Good documentation will assist in formulating claims after the clean up operation. The list of records to be maintained includes response work plans, daily reports of response work, progress made and the minutes of response planning meetings. Proper filling up of forms will help to ensure that claims against the offending vessels or parties are properly documented and authenticated. Port Master’s Office (Admin) is responsible for the overall documentation during the response operation.\
(2) Records shall at least focus on the following areas:\
(a) Type of operation\
(b) Utilization of manpower (number of hours worked, hourly rate and total cost)\
(c) Utilization of equipment and materials for the entire clean-up operation\
(d) Communication.\
(e) Names of government and commercial agencies supporting the operation\
(3) All parties incurring expenses in the clean-up and rescue/salvage operations shall maintain a true account of the expenses incurred and where possible bills must accompany all statements of account.\
(4) The settlement of the bills would depend on the circumstances of individual cases of operation and also on the result of legal process and civil action which would normally determine the possibility and extent of recovery of cost from the spiller.\
**b. International Compensation Regime.**\
(1) Under the 1992 Civil Liability Convention (CLC), claims for compensation for oil pollution damage caused by persistent oil may be made against the registered owner of the ship from which the oil that caused the damage originated (or his insurer). However, the shipowner can normally limit his financial liability to an amount determined by the size (tonnage) of the particular ship involved. The shipowner is obliged to maintain insurance to cover his liability under the Convention.\
(2) Under the 1992 Fund Convention, additional compensation is made available by the 1992 Fund when claimants do not obtain full compensation under the 1992 CLC. This can happen in the following cases:\
a. The damage exceeds the limit of the shipowner’s liability under the 1992 CLC.\
b. The shipowner is not liable under the 1992 CLC because the damage was caused either by a grave natural disaster, or wholly caused intentionally by a third party, or wholly caused as a result of the negligence of public authorities in maintaining lights or other navigational aids.\
c. The shipowner is financially incapable of meeting his obligations under the 1992 CLC in full, and the insurance is insufficient to pay valid compensation claims.\
(3) Claims under the 1992 CLC and Fund Convention 1992 should be presented clearly and with sufficient information and supporting documentation to enable the amount of the damage to be assessed. Each item of a claim must be substantiated by an invoice or other relevant supporting documentation, such as work sheets, explanatory notes, accounts and photographs. It is the responsibility of claimants to submit sufficient evidence to support their claims. It is important that the documentation is complete and accurate. If the documentation in support of a claim is likely to be considerable, claimants should contact the 1992 Fund (or where appropriate the designated surveyor or local claims office) as soon as possible after the incident to discuss claim presentation.\
(4) Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) between MPA and International Tanker Owners Pollution Federation Ltd (ITOPF). The MOU covers the pre-agreed schedule of ITOPF-endorsed Oil Spill Response (OSR) and vessel rates, which will help to expedite the claims settlement in the event of an oil pollution incident in Singapore’s waters, involving vessels covered by any of the International Group of P&I Club (IG P&I). MPA will gazette its rates for the provision of its OSR services, which include fire-fighting, hydrographic, garbage collection and patrol craft used for anti-oil pollution purposes. Where the oil spill response or anti-pollution services are provided by participating OSR agencies other than MPA, the charges payable will be computed according to the applicable rates set out in the Schedule to the MOU. All the MPA OSRA’s rates rates were published in MPA’s webpage www.mpa.gov.sg.\
c. Logistics Support\
(1) Respective agencies shall arrange adequate personnel to manage provision of stores and meals for their men during the clean-up operation particularly when the operation stretches more than a few days.\
(2) Bills of such costs shall be accurately accounted for and compiled for later submission to Port Master’s Office (Admin) for claims.

### 11. Risk Analysis
a. Within Singapore port limits, the historical data shows high spill frequencies from ships at container terminals and the anchorages. These were mostly minor spills due to leaks resulting from bunkering operations. However, MPA has handled three major oil spill incidents outside Singapore port limits to date. Evoikos incident on 15th Oct 1997 which resulted in 28,500 tonnes of oil spilled and the Natuna Sea incident on 3rd Oct 2000 which resulted in 7,000 tonnes of oil spilled. On the 25th May 2010, a collision between the bulk carrier “Waily” and the tanker “Bunga Kelana 3” resulted in estimated spill of 2500 tonnes of Bintulu Crude Oil into the waters of the Singapore Strait.\
b. These major incidents occurred outside Singapore port limits in the Traffic Separation Scheme and Precautionary Areas where the confluence of shipping traffic is at its greatest. Given the geographical nature of the Singapore Strait and the prevailing shipping routes, the Traffic Separation Scheme and Precautionary Areas are deemed to be of higher risk for an oil spill resulting from marine accidents. Refer to Annex D for the chartlet on high risk areas.\
c. Based on likely shipping movement in the Singapore Strait, it is assessed that the worst case oil spill scenario could be the collision between two fully laden VLCCs which could result in 30,000 tonnes of spilled crude oil.\
- Quantity of oil spilled is based on the assessment that a collision between two VLCCs will result in the breach of two wing cargo oil tanks with approximate capacity of 15,000 tonnes each.

### 13. Health & Safety Policy.
All responders should be aware that at all times human life, health and safety is paramount. The degree of risk associated with the clean-up operations will depend on the type of oil, size and location of the spill, circumstances of the spill, and weather conditions. Fresh crude oil and refined petroleum products are capable of giving off flammable gases. Therefore, fire and explosion remain a real danger to personnel and equipment. At all times, all responders should be aware of the limitations and safe operating procedures for all equipment used throughout the phases of the clean-up operation. Individual agencies are to appoint a safety officer to address the safety requirements. This should, where necessary, include a risk assessment and development of a formal site-specific management plan, including details for induction and briefing procedures.
